## ü§∑ **Maybe the Problem is Deeper**

### **Are There Really Clean Boundaries?**

When we analyze text, we're often doing multiple things simultaneously:

1. **Describing** what's there linguistically
2. **Interpreting** what it means  
3. **Inferring** what it tells us about the world
4. **Predicting** what effects it will have
5. **Evaluating** how well it achieves goals
6. **Explaining** why it was crafted this way

These seem to **blend together** rather than existing as distinct categories.

### **Example: "Don't let them take away your Social Security"**

**What am I doing when I analyze this?**
- Describing: Uses imperative mood, possession language, threat framing
- Interpreting: Means "oppose benefit cuts"
- Inferring: Suggests benefit cuts are being proposed
- Predicting: Will likely increase opposition to cuts
- Evaluating: Effective messaging strategy
- Explaining: Uses loss frame because loss aversion makes it more powerful

**These all seem interconnected** - each builds on the others.

## üîß **Different Possible Dimensions**

### **Object of Analysis**
- Text properties (linguistic features)
- World properties (what text describes)
- Communication properties (strategic choices)
- Effect properties (audience responses)

### **Temporal Orientation**
- Past: Why was text created this way?
- Present: What does text currently contain/mean?
- Future: What will text do/cause?

### **Level of Analysis**
- Surface: What's explicitly stated
- Deep: What's implied or suggested
- Meta: What the existence of this text tells us

### **Purpose of Analysis**
- Academic understanding
- Strategic evaluation  
- Practical application
- Critical assessment

## üí≠ **Maybe the Real Issue**

The analytic tiers (descriptive, explanatory, predictive, prescriptive) might not correspond to **natural joints in reality**. 

Maybe what we're really doing is **asking different questions** that don't fall into neat categories:

- What does this text say?
- What does this text mean?
- Why does this text exist?
- What will this text do?
- How good is this text at its purpose?
- What does this text tell us about the world?
- How should we respond to this text?

And different **theories help us answer different combinations** of these questions, rather than fitting into predetermined analytic boxes.
